Project Context

Setting the stage

Spur is a decentralized education platform where creators can build and sell courses, and learners can discover and consume content. I joined as a product designer to help scale the platform's growth — starting with the very first experience a user has: signing up.

The onboarding flow was one of the most critical surfaces on the product. A bad first impression meant immediate churn. A good one meant a user who understood the platform and stayed.

Problem Definition

Who are we designing for?

🎓

Persona 01

The Creator

Educators, influencers, and subject-matter experts who want to monetize their expertise. They're tech-savvy and motivated, but time-crunched — and they need to trust the platform before they invest in it.

Tech-savvy Monetization-focused Time-crunched Expert-driven
📚

Persona 02

The Learner

Professionals, hobbyists, and students who want to consume content and grow. They have varying technical ability and are driven by curiosity or career advancement — but are easily deterred by friction.

Varying tech ability Goal-oriented Curious Friction-sensitive
"How might we alleviate the confusion between the two user types throughout the sign-up onboarding process?"

Project Goals

1

Reduce drop-off during sign-up by clarifying the user type distinction early — before users commit to a path they don't understand.

2

Progressive disclosure — only ask for what's needed, when it's needed. Reduce cognitive load by staging the form across focused steps.

3

Help users experience the platform's core value before completing registration — building trust before demanding commitment.

Research

Understanding the landscape

Current Sign-Up & Login Flow

Before designing, I mapped the existing flow end-to-end. The current sign-up process mixed Creator and Learner paths from the very first screen — a single form with no context about what each account type meant.

Users had to choose between "Sign up as Creator" or "Sign up as Learner" with no explanation of the difference, leading to confusion and abandoned sessions.

Current Flow Problems

❌

No explanation of account types at the point of selection. Users were expected to self-identify without any product context.

❌

Identical form fields shown to both user types — no personalization, no signal that the platform understood the user's goal.

❌

Password requirements only shown after error, not upfront. This created frustration and interrupted the flow at a critical moment.

Ideating & Wireframing

Exploring directions

I explored 3 distinct wireframe directions before landing on the final approach. Each addressed the core problem differently — forcing me to stress-test the assumptions behind each model.

Direction 01 — Split Screen Entry

❌ The split screen forced a binary choice too early. Users with no context about account types were still confused — they'd seen the labels, but not what they meant in practice.

Direction 02 — Unified Sign Up + Type Selector After

❌ Collecting credentials before the user understood the account type meant the decision still felt arbitrary. The sequence created a cognitive mismatch — users committed before they had context.

Direction 03 — Value-first Entry with Progressive Disclosure

✓ Starting with just an email, then presenting account types with clear descriptions, significantly reduced cognitive load. Users had context before making the decision. This was the direction worth developing.

Design Decisions

Crafting the final flow

The final design implemented the value-first, progressive disclosure model from Direction 03. Each decision was intentional and grounded in research findings.

01

Type Selection Screen — Context Before Choice

Adding short descriptions to each option — "Build and sell your courses" for Creator, "Discover and learn from creators" for Learner — reduced confusion measurably. Users now had context before choosing, turning a confusing binary into a meaningful decision.

02

Progressive Form — 3 Focused Steps

Breaking the form into 3 focused steps — Account → Profile → Interests — reduced perceived complexity significantly. Users could see exactly how far they had to go. Completion felt achievable. The progress indicator also gave users a sense of momentum, encouraging continued engagement.

03

Mobile-first Responsive Design

Since 60%+ of Spur's traffic came from mobile, all designs were mobile-first with the desktop adapting from that base — not the other way around. This meant type cards stacked vertically on small screens, touch targets were at least 44px tall, and form fields were optimized for virtual keyboard interactions.

Testing & Impact

What the numbers said

After shipping, I worked with the engineering team to instrument key funnel events and ran a 3-week A/B test comparing the old flow to the new. The results validated the design hypotheses across every key metric.

Sign-up Completion Rate

42% → 67%
↑ +25 percentage points

The biggest single jump — driven by the progressive disclosure model reducing abandonment mid-form.

Time to Complete Sign-up

4m 12s → 2m 38s
↓ −37% faster

Clearer structure and reduced cognitive load let users move through the flow with confidence.

Account Type Error Rate

22% → 6%
↓ −73% fewer errors

Users switching account type post-signup dropped dramatically — context-first selection worked.

7-Day Retention

31% → 48%
↑ +17 percentage points

Users who understood what they signed up for were significantly more likely to return within a week.

Test methodology: 3-week A/B test with 50/50 traffic split. New users randomized between existing flow (control) and redesigned flow (variant). Statistical significance reached at 95% confidence level by end of week 2.

Reflection

What I learned

"Onboarding is less about collecting information and more about building trust. Users don't abandon sign-ups because forms are hard — they abandon them because they don't yet believe the product is worth their time."

✦ Learnings & Successes

The biggest win wasn't the 25-point completion rate improvement — it was the reduction in account type confusion. That metric directly reflected how well we communicated the product's value proposition at the earliest possible moment.

Progressive disclosure as a design principle proved powerful not just for usability, but for trust-building. Each step felt like a conversation rather than an interrogation.

◈ Challenges

The hardest constraint was organizational: Spur's engineering team had limited bandwidth, so I had to design a flow that improved UX meaningfully without requiring a full backend restructure — working within the existing auth system and finding progressive disclosure solutions that were frontend-only.

Balancing the needs of two very different user types in a single flow without making the experience feel fragmented was the core design challenge. Finding the right moment to split the paths — after email, before password — was the key insight.

If I had more time…

→

Introduce a lightweight value preview — showing a sample course or creator dashboard before signup is complete, to demonstrate platform value even earlier in the funnel.

→

Build an onboarding checklist post-signup, personalized to user type — guiding Creators through course creation and Learners through discovery in their first session.

→

Run qualitative usability sessions with 5–8 users on the new flow to surface edge cases and microfriction points that quantitative data doesn't capture.
