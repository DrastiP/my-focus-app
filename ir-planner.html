IR Planner — Designing for High-Stakes Incident Response

2024 Product Design · UX Research Arctic Wolf Networks
UX Research Interaction Design Complex Systems Cybersecurity Design Systems
Overview

When seconds matter, bad UX costs more than time

Arctic Wolf's Incident Response (IR) Planner is the command centre for security analysts managing live cyber incidents. During an active breach, analysts need to coordinate containment actions, track investigation tasks, and communicate status to stakeholders — all simultaneously, under extreme pressure.

The existing tool was fragmented and cognitively demanding. Analysts were context-switching between multiple screens, missing task dependencies, and losing critical time to UI friction — exactly when they could least afford it.

I led the end-to-end redesign: from embedded research with the analyst team, through iterative concept exploration, to a final design system that shipped as part of Arctic Wolf's core platform.

The Problem

Chaos wasn't just a workflow problem — it was a design problem

Before designing anything, I needed to understand the world analysts actually worked in. I sat alongside two senior IR analysts during live incident simulations and conducted structured interviews with three others across different experience levels.

"I'm copying task statuses into a Slack thread because I can't trust the tool to show me what's actually happening right now."

— Senior IR Analyst, Arctic Wolf

Three themes emerged consistently across every session:

🧩
Fragmented context
Incident details, task lists, and stakeholder comms lived in different places. Analysts had to mentally reconstruct the full picture on every screen switch.
⏱️
No sense of urgency
The UI didn't communicate priority or time sensitivity. A critical containment task looked identical to a routine documentation step.
🔄
Invisible dependencies
Task sequences weren't surfaced. Analysts discovered blockers only after starting work — creating rework loops at the worst possible moments.
Research Findings

What analysts actually need under pressure

Incident response is a dual-track cognitive task: analysts need to execute precise technical steps while maintaining a high-level mental model of where the incident stands. Most tools optimise for one at the expense of the other.

01
Glanceable status over deep reads
During an active incident, analysts can't afford to read — they scan. Status needs to be communicable in under 2 seconds from anywhere in the tool.
02
Structured flexibility
Playbooks provide structure, but every incident is different. The tool needs to support templated workflows while allowing analysts to adapt on the fly.
03
Trust through transparency
Analysts lose confidence in tools that don't reflect real-time state. Stale data is worse than no data during an incident.
04
Coordination as first-class feature
IR is a team sport. The tool needs to make it obvious who owns what and flag when someone is blocked — without requiring a Slack thread to fill the gap.

I synthesised these findings into a design brief framed around one core principle: the tool should reduce the analyst's cognitive burden, not add to it. Every design decision would be tested against this.

Design Process

From 4 concepts to 1 direction — and the reasoning behind each decision

I ran two rounds of concept exploration: a broad divergent sprint generating four distinct approaches, followed by a focused convergent phase where I stress-tested each against the research findings. I brought mockups back to the analyst team at every major fork.

Key Decision #1
Should the incident view be single-panel or multi-panel?
Early concepts used tabbed panels to separate "tasks", "timeline", and "stakeholders". Testing revealed analysts were constantly switching tabs to cross-reference information — exactly the fragmentation they had in the old tool.

Decision: Moved to a persistent two-column layout with the incident summary always visible on the left. Right panel adapts contextually. This reduced tab-switching by keeping critical context on screen at all times.
Key Decision #2
How should task severity and urgency be communicated?
The old tool used text labels ("Critical", "High", "Medium") that analysts reported scanning past under pressure. I explored icon-only, colour-only, and combined systems.

Decision: Adopted a colour + position hierarchy: critical tasks are always surfaced to the top of the task list with a red left-border accent. Severity labels are retained but secondary. Tested with analysts at 2AM (simulating stress conditions) — recognition time dropped significantly compared to baseline.
Key Decision #3
How do we surface task dependencies without overwhelming analysts?
Full dependency graphs felt like adding complexity for analysts already at capacity. But hiding dependencies entirely meant they'd discover blockers too late.

Decision: Dependencies are surfaced only when they become relevant — a blocked task automatically shows a tooltip listing what it's waiting on. The full dependency chain is accessible via an "expand" interaction but stays collapsed by default. This follows the progressive disclosure principle: don't show complexity until it's needed.
Design Principles

The rules I designed by

These four principles emerged from research and governed every micro-decision in the final design — from component states to information hierarchy to interaction patterns.

01
Reduce before you reorganise
Every piece of information on screen has a cost. Before adding a new element, the first question is always: can this be communicated with something already there? The final design shows ~40% fewer UI elements than the original while surfacing more actionable information.
02
Design for the worst moment
Analysts aren't using this tool when they're calm and rested. Design for 2AM, under pressure, with three people asking for status updates. If an interaction requires conscious thought to parse, it's too complex for this context.
03
State is never ambiguous
Every element that can be in multiple states (tasks, incidents, users, connections) has a visually distinct representation for each state. There is no "I'm not sure if this is active or stale" — the design answers that question before it's asked.
04
Templates accelerate, never constrain
IR playbooks are starting points. The system supports templated task sequences while making deviation feel natural — not like breaking a rule. Every structured element has an obvious "customise this" affordance.
Before → After

What changed and why it mattered

The redesign wasn't about aesthetics — it was about rethinking the information model entirely. Here's how the experience shifted for analysts:

Before
  • Incident context buried under tabs; analysts rebuilt context on every switch
  • Task list showed all tasks equally — no visual prioritisation
  • Stakeholder updates required leaving the tool entirely
  • Task dependencies only visible in documentation, not the UI
  • Status was text-only; required reading to parse
After
  • Persistent incident summary always visible — context never leaves the screen
  • Critical tasks float to top with colour + border hierarchy for instant scan
  • Stakeholder status panel integrated directly into the incident view
  • Dependencies surfaced inline at the point of relevance
  • State communicated visually — colour, position, and iconography work together

The redesigned IR Planner shipped as part of Arctic Wolf's managed detection and response platform. Analyst feedback collected after rollout consistently cited the new task hierarchy and persistent incident context as the most impactful changes.

Beyond the shipped product, the design system patterns developed here — particularly the state visualisation approach and progressive disclosure of dependencies — were adopted across two additional features in the security operations workflow.

Reflections

What I'd do differently

🔬
More stress-condition testing, earlier
Some of the most useful validation came from testing in simulated high-pressure conditions. I'd build that in from concept phase, not just final validation — decisions look different when you're tired and distracted.
📐
Involve engineering in dependency decisions
The dependency surfacing pattern required more backend work than anticipated. Earlier engineering collaboration in the design phase would have let us scope a stronger MVP and avoid scope reduction late in the cycle.
📊
Define success metrics before shipping
We had qualitative feedback from analysts but limited quantitative instrumentation to measure things like task completion time or context-switch frequency post-launch. Setting up that measurement upfront would have made the impact story sharper.
🌀
The power of designing for edge cases first
Designing for 2AM under pressure forced me to solve the hardest version of the problem first. The "normal" use case got better as a result. This approach is something I now apply across every project — stress the design early.
